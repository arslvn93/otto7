# Real Estate Glossary — Plain Language Reference

Use this glossary when clients ask what a term means, or when you need to explain something in an email or conversation without sounding like a textbook.

---

## Transaction & Contract Terms

**Appraisal:** A professional estimate of a property's market value, ordered by the lender. The appraiser visits the home and compares it to recent sales. If the appraisal comes in lower than the purchase price, the deal may need to be renegotiated.

**Assessed Value:** The value your local government assigns to a property for tax purposes. This is often different from market value — sometimes significantly.

**Buyer's Agent:** The agent who represents the buyer in a transaction. They help find properties, write offers, and negotiate on the buyer's behalf.

**Listing Agent:** The agent who represents the seller. They market the property, advise on pricing, and negotiate offers on the seller's behalf.

**Closing:** The final step of a real estate transaction where ownership officially transfers from seller to buyer. Both parties sign paperwork, funds are transferred, and the buyer gets the keys.

**Closing Costs:** Fees and expenses beyond the property price that buyers and sellers pay at closing. Typically 2-5% of the purchase price for buyers. Includes lender fees, title insurance, escrow fees, prorated taxes, and more.

**Closing Disclosure (CD):** A document the buyer receives at least 3 business days before closing that outlines the final loan terms, monthly payment, and all closing costs.

**Contingency:** A condition in a purchase contract that must be met for the deal to proceed. Common contingencies include financing (buyer must secure a loan), inspection (buyer can negotiate repairs or walk away), and appraisal (the home must appraise at or above the purchase price).

**Counteroffer:** A response to an offer that changes one or more terms. When a seller counters, the original offer is dead — the buyer can accept, counter back, or walk away.

**Days on Market (DOM):** How many days a property has been actively listed for sale on the MLS. High DOM can signal overpricing. Low DOM usually means strong demand.

**Due Diligence:** The buyer's investigation period after going under contract. This is when inspections happen, title is searched, and the buyer verifies everything about the property.

**Earnest Money (EMD):** A deposit the buyer puts down when their offer is accepted, showing they're serious. Usually 1-3% of the purchase price. Held in escrow and applied toward closing costs or down payment.

**Escrow:** A neutral third party that holds funds and documents during a transaction. Also refers to the period between accepted offer and closing ("we're in escrow").

**Escalation Clause:** A clause in an offer that automatically increases the buyer's price by a set amount above competing offers, up to a maximum. Used in competitive situations.

**Title:** Legal ownership of a property. A title search checks for any liens, claims, or issues that could affect ownership.

**Title Insurance:** Insurance that protects the buyer (and lender) from any title defects discovered after closing — things like unknown liens, forged documents, or ownership disputes.

**Under Contract:** A property that has an accepted offer but hasn't closed yet. Also called "pending."

## Financing Terms

**Amortization:** How a loan is paid off over time through regular payments. Early payments are mostly interest; later payments are mostly principal.

**APR (Annual Percentage Rate):** The total yearly cost of a loan including interest and fees, expressed as a percentage. APR is always higher than the interest rate alone and gives a more complete picture of borrowing cost.

**Conventional Loan:** A mortgage not insured by a government agency. Usually requires higher credit scores and a larger down payment than FHA or VA loans, but avoids some of their restrictions and fees.

**Debt-to-Income Ratio (DTI):** Monthly debt payments divided by gross monthly income. Lenders typically want this below 43-45%. A buyer with $6,000/month income and $2,400 in total monthly debts (including the new mortgage) has a 40% DTI.

**Down Payment:** The cash portion the buyer pays upfront. Conventional loans can go as low as 3%, FHA as low as 3.5%, VA and USDA can be 0% down. Larger down payments mean lower monthly payments and potentially better rates.

**Equity:** The difference between what a home is worth and what's owed on it. A home worth $400K with a $300K mortgage has $100K in equity.

**FHA Loan:** A government-insured loan with lower credit and down payment requirements (3.5% down, credit scores as low as 580). Requires mortgage insurance premiums (MIP).

**Fixed-Rate Mortgage:** A loan where the interest rate stays the same for the entire term. The payment never changes (not counting taxes and insurance adjustments).

**Adjustable-Rate Mortgage (ARM):** A loan where the interest rate can change after an initial fixed period. A 5/1 ARM has a fixed rate for 5 years, then adjusts annually. Lower initial rates, but risk of payment increases later.

**Interest Rate:** The cost of borrowing money, expressed as a percentage. This is what the lender charges for the loan, not including fees (that's APR).

**Mortgage Insurance (PMI/MIP):** Insurance required when the buyer puts less than 20% down. Protects the lender if the borrower defaults. PMI is for conventional loans (can be removed at 20% equity); MIP is for FHA loans.

**Points (Discount Points):** Upfront fees paid to the lender to reduce the interest rate. One point = 1% of the loan amount. Paying points makes sense if you're keeping the home long enough to recoup the upfront cost through lower monthly payments.

**Pre-Approval:** A lender's conditional commitment to lend a specific amount, based on verified income, credit, and assets. Stronger than pre-qualification. Most sellers require a pre-approval letter with any offer.

**Pre-Qualification:** A rough estimate of how much a buyer can borrow, based on self-reported financial information. Less rigorous than pre-approval.

**VA Loan:** A mortgage guaranteed by the Department of Veterans Affairs for eligible veterans and active military. Allows 0% down payment and has no PMI requirement.

## Pricing & Market Terms

**Absorption Rate:** How fast homes are selling in a given area. Calculated by dividing the number of sales in a period by available inventory. Tells you whether you're in a buyer's, seller's, or balanced market.

**Broker Price Opinion (BPO):** An agent's estimate of a property's value. Less formal (and less expensive) than an appraisal. Often used by banks for foreclosures and short sales.

**Buyer's Market:** When there are more homes for sale than buyers. Gives buyers negotiating power. Typically defined as more than 6 months of inventory.

**Seller's Market:** When there are more buyers than homes available. Drives prices up and creates competition. Typically defined as less than 3 months of inventory.

**Comparable Sales (Comps):** Recently sold properties similar to the subject property in size, condition, location, and features. Used to determine market value for pricing and appraisals.

**CMA (Comparative Market Analysis):** A report an agent prepares showing comparable sales, active listings, and expired listings to recommend a listing price. Not the same as an appraisal but uses similar methods.

**Fair Market Value:** The price a willing buyer would pay and a willing seller would accept, with both having reasonable knowledge of the relevant facts and neither being under pressure to act.

**List Price vs. Sale Price:** List price is the asking price. Sale price is what the property actually sold for. The ratio between them indicates market conditions — above 100% means homes are selling over asking.

**Price Per Square Foot:** Sale price divided by livable square footage. Useful for comparing similar properties but can be misleading if homes have vastly different features, lot sizes, or conditions.

## Property & Inspection Terms

**As-Is:** The seller is selling the property in its current condition and will not make repairs. Buyers can still get inspections, but the seller isn't obligated to fix anything.

**Certificate of Occupancy (CO):** A document from the local government confirming a building meets code and is safe to inhabit. Required for new construction and sometimes for renovations.

**Clear to Close:** The lender has reviewed everything and the loan is approved. All conditions have been met and the deal is ready for closing day.

**Fixture:** Something permanently attached to the property that conveys with the sale (light fixtures, built-in shelving, ceiling fans). Fixtures stay unless specifically excluded in the contract.

**Home Inspection:** A professional examination of a property's condition — structure, roof, plumbing, electrical, HVAC, foundation, etc. Not required but strongly recommended for buyers.

**Home Warranty:** A service contract that covers repair or replacement of major home systems and appliances (HVAC, plumbing, electrical, kitchen appliances) for a set period, usually one year. Sellers sometimes offer this as a closing incentive.

**HOA (Homeowners Association):** An organization that manages a community (condo, townhome, or planned development) and enforces rules. Residents pay monthly or annual dues. HOA docs should be reviewed carefully before buying.

**Lot:** The land a property sits on. Lot size, shape, and position (corner lot, cul-de-sac, backing to open space) all affect value.

**MLS (Multiple Listing Service):** The database where agents list properties for sale. Feeds to Zillow, Realtor.com, Redfin, and other consumer sites. Only licensed agents can list on the MLS.

**Septic System:** A private sewage treatment system (no municipal sewer). Has a tank and drain field. Requires periodic pumping and inspection. Common in rural areas.

**Survey:** A map showing a property's boundaries, dimensions, and any encroachments or easements. Lenders often require one.

**Walk-Through:** The buyer's final inspection of the property, usually 24-48 hours before closing, to verify the home's condition and that any agreed-upon repairs were completed.

## Agent & Business Terms

**Brokerage:** The real estate company an agent works under. All agents must be licensed under a brokerage.

**Commission:** The fee paid for real estate services, typically a percentage of the sale price. Commission structures vary. Since the 2024 NAR settlement, buyer agent compensation is negotiated separately and must be agreed upon in writing before touring homes.

**Dual Agency:** When one agent (or brokerage) represents both the buyer and seller in the same transaction. Legal in some states with disclosure; prohibited in others. Creates inherent conflicts of interest.

**Fiduciary Duty:** An agent's legal obligation to act in their client's best interest. Includes loyalty, confidentiality, disclosure, obedience, accounting, and reasonable care.

**Lockbox:** A secure box on a listed property that holds the key. Allows authorized agents to access the home for showings. Electronic lockboxes track who entered and when.

**MLS Remarks (Agent Remarks vs. Public Remarks):** Agent remarks are notes visible only to other agents (showing instructions, commission info, access codes). Public remarks are the listing description visible to everyone.

**Open House:** A scheduled period where a listed property is open for potential buyers to tour without an appointment. Used for marketing exposure and lead generation.

**Pocket Listing:** A property for sale that isn't listed on the MLS. Also called an "off-market" listing. Less exposure but more privacy for the seller.
