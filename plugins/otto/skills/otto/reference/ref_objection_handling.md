# Objection Handling Scripts — Buyer & Seller

Use these scripts as frameworks when helping agents respond to common client pushback. Adapt the tone and specifics to each situation. The goal is never to "overcome" an objection with pressure — it's to address the real concern underneath it.

---

## BUYER OBJECTIONS

---

### "I want to wait for interest rates to drop."

**The real concern:** They're afraid of overpaying on monthly payments.

**Talking points:**
- Rates are unpredictable — even the Fed doesn't know exactly when or how much they'll move. Waiting is a gamble in both directions.
- When rates drop, buyer demand surges. More competition means higher prices, bidding wars, and fewer choices. You could end up paying more for the house to save less on the rate.
- You marry the house, you date the rate. If rates drop after you buy, you refinance. You keep the house you got at today's price with today's lower competition.
- Run the actual numbers: show them what the monthly payment looks like now versus what it might look like with a lower rate but a higher purchase price. Often it's a wash — or worse.

**Sample response:**
"I totally understand that concern. Here's what I'm seeing in the market right now: [current inventory data, competition level]. If rates drop even half a point, we historically see a flood of buyers re-enter the market. That means more competition for fewer homes, which pushes prices up. Let me model out two scenarios for you — buying now versus waiting six months — so you can see the real numbers side by side."

---

### "I want to keep looking / I'm not ready to make an offer."

**The real concern:** Fear of making the wrong decision. Or they haven't found "the one."

**Talking points:**
- Acknowledge that this is a huge decision and it's smart to be thoughtful.
- Gently check: what specifically is missing? Is there a feature they're holding out for that may not exist in their budget?
- If they've been looking for a long time, reset expectations with data. Show them what's actually available in their price range and area.
- If the home checks most of their boxes, remind them that no home is perfect — and the longer they search, the more likely they are to lose good options.

**Sample response:**
"There's no pressure at all — this is your decision and I want you to feel great about it. Can I ask, though: what's the gap between this home and what you're picturing? Because I want to make sure we're calibrated on what's realistic in your price range so we're not passing up a great fit while chasing something that might not exist in this market."

---

### "I found a home on Zillow / Redfin. Can I just call the listing agent?"

**The real concern:** They don't see the value of buyer representation, or they think going direct saves money.

**Talking points:**
- The listing agent works for the seller. Their job is to get the highest price and best terms for the seller — not the buyer.
- Having your own agent means someone is negotiating FOR you, reviewing contracts, catching red flags in inspections, and managing the entire process.
- Going direct doesn't save the buyer money in most cases. The commission structure is between the seller and their agent — it doesn't change because you don't have representation.
- Explain your value: the homes you find before they hit portals, the off-market opportunities, the negotiation strategy, the vendor network, the problem-solving when issues come up.

**Sample response:**
"Great eye — that's a nice property. Here's something to keep in mind: the listing agent's job is to represent the seller's interests and get them the highest price. If you reach out directly, there's nobody in your corner. I can get you into that home, plus I'll pull comps to make sure we offer the right price, negotiate repairs after inspection, and handle everything through closing. My job is to save you money and protect you — and it doesn't cost you extra to have that."

---

### "The home needs too much work."

**The real concern:** Overwhelmed by the scope of updates, or worried about costs they can't see.

**Talking points:**
- Separate cosmetic from structural. Paint, flooring, and fixtures are relatively cheap and easy. Foundation, roof, and plumbing are expensive and important.
- Get contractor estimates before walking away. The actual cost is often less than what people imagine.
- Homes that need work often sell below comps. The discount might more than cover the renovation. Do the math together.
- Consider renovation loan options (FHA 203k, Fannie Mae HomeStyle) that roll the cost of improvements into the mortgage.

**Sample response:**
"I hear you — it can look overwhelming at first glance. But let me ask: is it the kitchen and bathrooms that bother you, or is it bigger stuff like the roof and mechanicals? Because if it's cosmetic, we can get a contractor estimate and compare this home's price to the move-in-ready comps. A lot of times, you come out ahead buying the 'ugly' house and making it yours."

---

### "We need to sell our current home first."

**The real concern:** Financial risk of carrying two mortgages, or needing the equity for the down payment.

**Talking points:**
- This is common and there are several strategies: sale contingency, bridge loan, HELOC for the down payment, or listing their home first and then making an offer.
- In a strong market, recommend listing first — their home may sell fast and then they're in a strong position to buy with no contingency.
- In a slower market, a sale contingency is reasonable and many sellers will accept one, especially if the buyer's home is priced well and likely to sell.
- Some lenders offer programs that allow buying before selling with deferred payments on the first mortgage.

**Sample response:**
"That's a really common situation and we have a few options. The strongest move is usually to list your home first — in this market, well-priced homes in [their area] are moving in [X days]. Once we're under contract on your sale, you're in a strong position to buy because sellers see you as a serious, funded buyer. Let me pull some comps on your current home so we can map out the timeline."

## SELLER OBJECTIONS

---

### "I want to price it higher than your recommendation."

**The real concern:** Emotional attachment, perceived value, or they need a certain number to make their next move work.

**Talking points:**
- Start with empathy. They've lived there, improved it, raised a family there. It's personal. But the market doesn't pay for memories — it pays based on what similar homes have sold for.
- Show the data. Walk through comps one by one. Point out the homes that priced high and sat, versus the ones that priced right and sold fast (often for more than asking).
- Explain the cost of overpricing: the first 2-3 weeks get the most attention. If the home is overpriced, it misses the best buyers. After DOM climbs, buyers wonder what's wrong with it.
- Offer a compromise: "Let's try it at [slightly above your recommendation] for 2 weeks, and if we don't have strong activity, we adjust. But I'd rather price it right from day one."

**Sample response:**
"I completely understand — you've put a lot into this home and it shows. Let me walk you through what similar homes in the neighborhood actually sold for in the last 90 days, because that's what buyers and their lenders are going to compare us to. [Walk through comps.] If we price at $[higher number], we're competing against homes that offer [more features]. If we price at $[your recommendation], we position ourselves to attract multiple offers, which often drives the price above asking. Would you be open to starting at $[compromise] and reviewing after the first two weekends?"

---

### "I'm not ready to sell yet / I want to wait for a better market."

**The real concern:** Timing anxiety, not sure where they'd go, or hoping for higher prices.

**Talking points:**
- Ask what "better market" means to them. More money? Fewer days on market? Both?
- If they're waiting for prices to rise: in most markets, the home they're buying next is also rising. If they sell higher, they buy higher. It often cancels out.
- If they're waiting for more inventory (to buy): they might be right. But their home also has to compete with that new inventory when they list.
- If they're just not ready emotionally or logistically: respect that. Don't push. Stay in touch and be the agent they call when they are ready.

**Sample response:**
"That makes total sense — timing matters. Can I ask what you're waiting for specifically? Because one thing I see a lot is sellers waiting for prices to go up, but then the home they want to buy has gone up too. The math usually works out about the same. What I can do right now is run a market analysis so you know exactly where you stand, and then we can check in quarterly to see if the timing feels right. No pressure at all."

---

### "Another agent said they could get me a higher price."

**The real concern:** They're being told what they want to hear, or they want to test your confidence.

**Talking points:**
- The agent who tells you the highest price doesn't get you the highest price — the market does.
- Ask: "Did they show you the comps that support that number? I'd love to see them."
- Explain "buying the listing" — some agents deliberately quote a high price to win the listing, then pressure the seller to reduce later. The seller wastes weeks or months.
- Your value isn't in quoting a big number. It's in pricing strategy, marketing execution, negotiation, and actually getting to closing.

**Sample response:**
"I'd want to see what data they're using to support that number, because I want to make sure I'm not missing something. Here are the comps I based my recommendation on — [walk through them]. If they have a comp I missed, I'm happy to adjust. But what I've seen happen too often is an agent quotes a high price to win the listing, the home sits, the seller reduces twice, and they end up selling for less than they would have if they'd priced it right on day one. I'd rather be honest with you upfront than waste your time."

---

### "I don't want to pay that much in commission."

**The real concern:** They feel the fee is too high relative to the perceived work, or they're trying to maximize proceeds.

**Talking points:**
- Don't get defensive. Acknowledge that it's a lot of money and they should understand exactly what they're getting.
- Walk through everything you do: pricing strategy, professional photography, staging consultation, MLS listing, syndication to hundreds of sites, open houses, showing coordination, offer review, negotiation, transaction management through closing, vendor coordination, and problem-solving when things go sideways.
- Explain that the buyer's agent compensation matters too. If it's too low, fewer agents will show the property. Fewer showings = fewer offers = lower price.
- Frame the cost against the outcome: "My goal is to net you more money in your pocket, even after my fee, than you'd get trying to save on commission."

**Sample response:**
"That's a fair question and I want you to feel good about the investment. Here's what I can tell you: my average listing sells in [X days] and at [X%] of list price. When you factor in my marketing, staging support, negotiation, and the problems I solve behind the scenes, my clients consistently net more than they would with a discount option — even after paying my fee. Let me show you a breakdown of everything included and then you can decide if the value is there."

---

### "I want to try selling it myself first (FSBO)."

**The real concern:** Want to save on commission, or believe their home will sell itself.

**Talking points:**
- Respect their decision. Don't be condescending about FSBOs.
- Share the data: FSBO homes statistically sell for less than agent-represented homes (NAR data). The commission savings are often wiped out by the lower sale price.
- Highlight what they'll need to handle: pricing, marketing, photography, showings, screening buyers, negotiating, contracts, inspections, appraisal issues, title, and closing coordination.
- Offer value: "Let me provide a free market analysis so you at least know your starting point. And if you decide you want help later, I'm here."

**Sample response:**
"I respect that — you know your home better than anyone. A couple things I'd want you to have going in: homes sold by owner tend to sell for meaningfully less than agent-listed homes, often more than enough to offset the commission. The other challenge is exposure — your home won't be on the MLS, which means it won't hit Zillow, Realtor.com, or Redfin the same way. If you want, I can put together a market analysis for free so you're pricing from real data. And if anything changes down the road, I'd love the chance to earn your business."

---

### "I need to sell by a specific date."

**The real concern:** Job relocation, school enrollment, divorce, financial pressure, or another time-sensitive situation.

**Talking points:**
- Take the deadline seriously. Work backward from their date and build a realistic timeline.
- Price aggressively from day one. If speed is the priority, the pricing strategy changes. You need to attract offers immediately, not wait for the market to catch up.
- Accelerate the prep: get photography, staging, and any minor repairs done ASAP. Launch in the strongest possible position.
- Have a Plan B conversation: if it doesn't sell in time, are they open to renting it out? Doing a lease-back? Accepting a lower price?

**Sample response:**
"Got it — [date] is the deadline and we'll work backward from there. That gives us [X weeks] to prep and list, and [X weeks] on market before we'd need an accepted offer to close on time. Here's what I'd recommend: we price it at $[competitive price] to generate immediate interest, we get professional photos done this week, and we launch by [date]. I'd also want to talk about a Plan B just so we're covered — but the goal is to get this done on your timeline."

## HOW TO USE THESE SCRIPTS

These are frameworks, not word-for-word recitations. When the agent describes a situation:

1. Identify the objection category
2. Consider what the REAL concern is underneath the stated objection
3. Adapt the talking points to the specific client and market context
4. Adjust the tone — some clients want data, some want empathy, some want directness
5. Always end with a next step or question that moves the conversation forward

Never use high-pressure tactics. The best agents earn trust by being honest, even when honesty means saying "you might be right" or "let's wait."
